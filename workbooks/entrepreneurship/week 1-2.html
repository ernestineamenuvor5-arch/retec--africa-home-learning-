Observing Needs vs. Wants

Learning Goal
Today, I will learn the difference between needs and wants, and understand why this matters for my business.

Quick Definitions

Need: Something people must have to survive or live comfortably (food, water, shelter, medicine, education).
Want: Something people would like to have but can live without (toys, ice cream, jewelry, fancy clothes).
Market Demand: How many people need or want what you plan to sell.
Discovery Steps
ENGAGE - Think & Discuss

Look at these items. Which are NEEDS and which are WANTS?

  • Pure water
  • Mobile phone
  • Rice and stew
  • PlayStation game
  • School uniform
  • Designer sneakers
  • Medicine for malaria
  • Chocolate
EXPLORE - My Activity Choices
Option A:
Create a table with two columns: NEEDS | WANTS. List at least 5 items in each column from your daily life.
Option B:
Interview 3 people in your home or neighborhood. Ask: "What do you need most right now?" Write their answers.
EXPLAIN - Learn Together

Why This Matters for Business:

Selling NEEDS = More customers, steady sales (people must buy)
Selling WANTS = Fewer customers, but can charge more (people buy when they have extra money)

Example:

  • A pure water seller will always have customers (NEED)
  • A video game shop might struggle if people don't have extra money (WANT)

Smart Business Tip: Many successful businesses turn WANTS into NEEDS!

  • Mobile phones were once a WANT, now they are a NEED for business
  • Internet was once a WANT, now it's a NEED for education
üí°
Tip:
Start with NEEDS to build a strong business. Add WANTS when your business is stable!
ELABORATE - Imagine & Share

Think about your business idea from Week 1:

  • Does it solve a NEED or a WANT?
  • Will people always buy it, or only when they have extra money?
  • Can you adjust your idea to solve a NEED?

Collecting Data from Customers

Simple Surveys
Learning Goal
Today, I will learn how to ask questions, collect information from people, and use data to make better business decisions.

Quick Definitions

Data: Information collected from people through questions, observations, or counting.
Survey: A set of questions you ask people to learn what they think or need.
Tally: A way to count answers by making marks (||||).
Bar Graph: A chart that uses bars to show how many people chose each answer.

Why Collect Data?

  • Know what customers really want (not just what you think they want)

How to Create a Simple Survey:

  1. Write a clear question

  5. Make your decision based on results

Turning Data Into Ideas

Brainstorming Solutions
Learning Goal
Today, I will use the data I collected to generate smart business ideas that match what people really need.

Quick Definitions

Brainstorming: Thinking of many different ideas quickly without judging them.
Business Opportunity: A need or problem you discovered that you can solve for money.
Product-Market Fit: When what you sell matches exactly what customers want to buy.
Discovery Steps
ENGAGE - Review Your Data

Look at the survey results from yesterday (yours or Kwame's example):

  • What did most people choose?
  • Were you surprised by any answers?
  • What does this tell you about what to sell?
EXPLORE - My Activity Choices
Option A:
Based on your survey data, brainstorm 3 different business ideas. For each idea, write what you will sell, why people will buy it, and how much it might cost to start.
Option B:
Create a "Problem-Solution Map" ‚Äî draw or write 3 problems people mentioned in your survey, and next to each problem, write your business solution.
EXPLAIN - Learn Together

From Data to Business Idea:

Step 1: Look at your survey results ‚Äî what got the most votes?

Step 2: Ask yourself:

  • Can I afford to make/buy this?
  • Do I have the skills to provide this?
  • Will enough people buy it to make profit?

Step 3: Choose the best idea based on:

  • Highest demand (most people want it)
  • Lowest cost to start
  • Easiest for you to do

Example:

Survey Result: 15 out of 20 students want cold drinks during break time.

Business Ideas:

  1. Sell Fan Ice (cheap to buy, easy to carry)
  2. Sell bottled water (everyone needs it)
  3. Make fresh sobolo (takes time but higher profit)

Best Choice for a beginner: Fan Ice or water (simple, low cost, high demand)

üí°
Tip:
Don't try to sell everything! Focus on the ONE thing people want most.
ELABORATE - Test Your Idea

Before you start, ask yourself:

  1. Demand Test: Did at least half the people say they would buy this?
  2. Money Test: Do I have enough money to start?
  3. Skill Test: Do I know how to make or get this product?
  4. Time Test: Can I do this while still going to school?

If you answered YES to most questions, your idea is ready to test!

Creating My Business Opportunity Map

Learning Goal
Today, I will organize all my findings into a clear Business Opportunity Map that shows who my customers are, what they need, and how I will help them.

Quick Definitions

Target Customer: The specific group of people who will buy from you (students, mothers, traders, children).
Value Proposition: The special reason why people should buy from YOU and not someone else.
Competition: Other people or businesses selling similar products.
Discovery Steps
ENGAGE - Think About Your Customer

Close your eyes and imagine your first customer:

  • How old are they?
  • What problem do they have?
  • Why will they choose to buy from you?
  • How much money can they spend?
EXPLORE - My Activity Choices
Option A:
Fill out the Business Opportunity Map template on the next page with your business idea.
Option B:
Draw a picture of your ideal customer and write a story about how your business solves their problem.
EXPLAIN - Learn Together

The 5 Key Questions Every Business Must Answer:

  1. WHO are my customers? (students, workers, mothers, youth)
  2. WHAT do they need? (food, water, clothes, entertainment, services)
  3. WHY will they buy from me? (cheaper, faster, better quality, closer)
  4. WHERE will I sell? (school, market, neighborhood, online)
  5. HOW MUCH will they pay? (GH‚Çµ 1, GH‚Çµ 5, GH‚Çµ 10)

Example: Ama's Pure Water Business

  • WHO: Students at her school (ages 12-16)
  • WHAT: Cold pure water during break time
  • WHY: She keeps water in a cooler (others sell warm water)
  • WHERE: At school during break and lunch
  • HOW MUCH: GH‚Çµ 0.50 per sachet
üí°
Tip:
The more specific you are about WHO you serve, the better you can serve them!

  • What do they do badly?
  • How can you be DIFFERENT or BETTER?
